Reflection Part One

Date: May 13th 2021

Name: Mackenzie Stack

Before starting this course, I would not be able to tell you exactly what "digital history" is or what "digital silences" are. My best guess would be that it is digitized history, primary and secondary documents uploaded to a website for public viewing. Upon completing this week's activities and readings, I've come to find that digital history is so much more than that. Digital history is a field in its own right, including the presentation and analysis of history that aims to widen the scope of engagement and promote thoughtful discussion. I found this week's readings helpful in demystifying a field that sits at the intersection of technology and history. I also found that, like many fields, digital history suffers from non-inclusivity that silences important voices.

In "Returning Women to the History of Digital History,"[^1] Sharon Leon demonstrates that women have been integral in the birth of digital history. Yet, they lack recognition due to permeating systemic failures in academic systems. Leon points to the work of important female digital historians such as Ayers, Brier, and Brown, who undeniably contributed to the field. If women are so integral, they indeed should be receiving more credit.

Luckily advocates are pushing for more inclusivity. In 'Clio, Rewired: Propositions for the Future of Digital History Pedagogy in Canada,'[^2] Kimberley Martin postulates solution's to develop digital history into a more inclusive field. As a professor, Martin palaces emphasis on pedagogy, explaining that modifying syllabi to include more textbooks and supplemental content written by women digital historians. In addition, Martin suggests widening the scope of content to include literary, queer, and oral content. I couldn't agree with Martin and Leon more as a female student.

I am very excited to work with technology and history. While the two readings I have reflected on focus on inclusivity in the field, they also detail some of the projects that people have worked on. Before this week, I had no idea people were creating exciting ways of presenting and analyzing history through digital methods. I am particularly intrigued by the oral history projects and projects like the "Great Unsolved Mysteries in Canadian History."[^3]

My background includes some technical ability, particularly in mobile applications. Although I don't know python, I've learned from learning other programming languages that it's just a matter of putting the time in. I will spend more time reviewing digital history projects, but I gather there might be some room here for a mobile application project. I recognize that this is not the goal of the course, but I think I've been inspired; perhaps a side project is on the horizon.

Reflection Part Two

Date: May 23rd 2022

Name: Mackenzie Stack

 The rather trite phrase “History is written by the Victors” often attributed to Churchill, has new meaning in the context of digital history. As the technological revolution changes how we interact with information, the new digital narrative of our history is being constructed, and digital historians are the architects and the victors in many ways. Prosaic textbooks and standard definition documentaries with corrupt audio are no longer the frontiers of presenting and doing history. Churchill’s quote is implying the winners in history often control the narrative. While this remains partly true, it’s the digital historians who are producing accessible versions of history enjoyed by the public. Another trite phrase that Churchill also happens to use comes to mind, “Where there is great power there is great responsibility.” (Although made famous by spiderman.)[^1]

 It’s interesting to consider narrative in the context of digital history. The narrative is one of the big separators between the computer and us. Even the closest computer programs to humans, such as artificial intelligence, care little about connecting to an overarching narrative. I think context and narrative are primary drivers of our human networked intelligence. If you look at the Transcribe Bentham project, the introduction of correspondence letters caused an uptick in transcribing activities among volunteers.[^2] They had what most of Bentham’s work lacked, a story. Without devolving into a philosophical discussion, I believe that we have so much trust in computers because they lack this need for meaning that’s causal in bias. Or they seem to, which is precisely the problem.

 The digital historian can write a program that utilizes some mystical technical tool and produce a seemingly unbiased history because the assumption is that the computer is infallible. Humanity trusts math and, by extension, the machine. However, we neglect to question who directs the machine. Who is paying the digital historian to produce work to end up online? Whose goals are baked into the programming parameters that produce the result? Who is the digital historian doing the work? What are their own beliefs and ideals that direct the machine for analysis? These are considerations we need to take into account when reviewing digital history. Perhaps, even more, than we do in traditional history.

 The digital historian has more responsibility than traditional historians because their audience is more extensive and not limited to trained academics. It’s one thing to expect a trained academic to be skeptical and thorough in their examination, but it’s another to expect this from the regular Twitter user. This is why we need to be obvious and transparent about data in its presentation. It is far more likely for a visually appealing chart to mass exposure on social networks than a link to a 200-page Ph.D. dissertation.

 Considering the colonial reports in the newspaper’s tutorial, Edinburgh and Glasgow are shaping the narrative.[^3] We also know there was a spelling mistake that would increase publications associated with Edinburgh if we opted to correct it. It’s not difficult to imagine on a larger scale; had we not opted to correct the spelling error, the data would have been misinterpreted. Perhaps Edinburgh would seemingly have less of a narrative shaping effect than is accurate. This choice is up to the digital historian, who can influence the whole by a technical choice, even on a micro-scale. This may seem like a stretch, but how many people review the source code. If we surveyed most readers, I believe we would find that the answer is not many and that they trust the machine to produce accurate results.

 Despite potential ethical issues, the value of digital history is understated. The traditional historian brings a trained eye and produces scholarly work that shapes our collective narrative of history. The digital historian brings the same trained eye that does academic work but reaches more people and accesses more documents and primary artifacts using modern tools. The Transcribe Bentham project did more than produce a cost-efficient way of digitizing work. Notably, the project meaningfully engaged the public, increasing awareness of Bentham’s work. [^4]

 What is a narrative without readers? Digital history connects with the public in a way that traditional accounts simply cannot. Digital history exposes and inspires more people to engage with historical work, achieving a goal at the heart of the history discipline. Technology is purposively filled with buzzwords that thwart users from further investigation. It is the task of digital historians not to fall victim to this industry standard. The ethical dimensions need to be considered, and efforts to find ways to make digital methods and tools more transparent need to happen. Ultimately, not using modern technology to improve history because of complex ethics is a mistake. Another Churchill quote, “To improve is to change; to be perfect is to change often.”
